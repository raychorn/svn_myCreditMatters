4 scary new ways creditors gouge you

If you snooze, you can lose -- by getting hit with fees, fees and more fees. With
delinquencies up about 30%, credit-card issuers are trying to take more money
out of your wallet. Here's how to protect yourself.  By Liz Pulliam Weston

"Can they really do that?"

Such is the anguished cry that ends many a reader's e-mail about some new
trick his credit-card issuer has pulled. The sign of the time is, unfortunately,
"yes." But not always.

Knowing what creditors can and can't do -- and what new maneuvers are
invented to drain your wallet -- can help you stay ahead in the credit world.

Among the trends at credit-card companies: more and bigger fees from
proliferation of penalty interest rates and new hidden costs.

Recently, I've heard from readers and industry experts about even more
fastballs that creditors are whizzing at their customers.

You may be at particular risk if you have spotty credit. Creditors are penalizing
people who might default now that credit-card delinquencies are up 30% in the
past two years and bankruptcy filings are at an all-time high.

But you could also be penalized for simply not paying attention. You need to
know about what's going on now.

Old debts come back to life
John Watters of Davie, Fla., was delighted to get a low-rate offer. After all, his
credit wasn't the greatest, thanks in part to credit-card bills he failed to pay to his
previous credit-card company.

Imagine his surprise, then when that $1,500 debt showed up on his new card.

Watters insists he didn't know he was "reaffirming," or agreeing to this debt,
when he signed up for the new card. The new credit-card company says the deal
was spelled out in the solicitation Watters was sent.

"I've seen the solicitation, and it's pretty clear," the credit-card spokesman said.

Watters and the new credit-card company are still wrangling over whether he'll
have to pay back old debt from another creditor and trying to entice borrowers of
the debt with new terms has become increasingly common another credit expert
said.

It's perfectly legal as long as the credit-card company is upfront that with the new
card comes an old debt, said lawyer Deanne Loonin.

"Simply slapping it on to a new debt without authorization and without warning,"
Loonin said, " could potentially violate a number of state laws."

Old debt reported as new
Unscrupulous collection agencies sometimes do this as a way of getting
delinquent borrowers to repay, but it's illegal. They usually pick debt about to be
dropped from the borrowers' credit reports under the Fair Credit Reporting Act's
seven-year statute of limitations. By reporting it as up-to-date, the debt could
remain on the report for another seven years.

That's what happened to Beth, a reader who asked that her last name wasn't
used. Six years and 10 months after an unresolved dispute with her company
first appeared on her credit report, a collection agency took her debt and
reposted it on her credit report with a new date, telling her it would continue to
be reported unless she paid the $200 balance.

The Federal Trade Commission, which regulates credit bureaus, stated clear
this tactic isn't allowed, Loonin said. Consumers facing this should demand the
credit bureaus investigate and correct the error. If the creditor refuses to back
down and continues to report the borrower can sue. Attorneys who represent
such cases can be found through the National Association of Consumer
Advocates.
Abrupt changes on longtime accounts
Another reader got slapped with a late fee recently after sending in payment.
After re-examining her statement, she noticed her grace time she had to pay her
bill before interest charges and late fees suddenly narrowed from 25 days to 20.

That's just one of the ways credit-card companies are changing these borrowers

periods and other features, the rate legally can be changed with just a few days'
notice.

Even the type of card you have is subject to change. Seeking for themselves,
some banks are switching their customers MasterCard-branded plastic to Visa,
or vice versa -- altering many terms along the way.

One reader whose Visa was replaced with a MasterCard discovered he was no
longer allowed to view or pay his bill online. Now instead of paying before the
statement closed, he has to wait for the statement to arrive and pay by check --
a process that takes days longer and increases the amount he ultimately pays.

The somewhat good news: Your credit-card issuer is required to print changes in
advance, and you're usually not obligated to accept if you're willing to give up
the card. In the fine-print brochure you get the changes, you're typically allowed
to decline the new terms and account under the old terms -- but you're not
allowed to keep using the card.

Really, really bad deals to people with spotty credit
For a few years in the mid- to late- 1990s, people with credit troubles were
themselves among the most sought-after customers in the credit world.

This topsy-turvy situation happened because the creditors decided to make lots
of money charging high interest rates to these risky customers. It became
relatively easy to get for what are known as "subprime" by those with credit
scores under 560 or so.

The inevitable happened, of course, the economy soured. Lenders took on too
much risk, delinquency rates spiked and the easy-credit ended.

Here are some ways you can reduce your chances of getting burned.
  • Read everything. Examine everything you receive in the mail from your
    credit-card company. Those brochures or slips of paper could lead to
    significant changes.
  • Improve your credit standing. People with good credit get the best
    deals -- and have the most leverage in getting card issuers to waive fees,
    change terms and otherwise alter terms in their favor. Learn More, take
    tour of myCredit assist.
  • Pay off your debts. Not only will paying off your credit cards improve
    your credit score, but it will give your credit-card issuers fewer ways to
    sting you since you won't be paying interest or overlimit fees.
